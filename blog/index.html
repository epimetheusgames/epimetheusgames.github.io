Creating Large Swarms of Fish

By Carson B: December 23, 2024

This blog post is going to cover my process for creating large swarms of fish for our game. In the ocean, there are many types of fish that create large schools. The reason for this is usually for safety and for finding food. Interestingly, schooling can improve the hydrodynamics of the fish, although I don't really understand why.

The main topic of this blog post is going to explore the technical side of creating performant large swarms of fish.

Often in computing we find that extremely simple sets of rules (such as Conway's Game of Life or the Mandelbrot Set) can create extremely complex and beautiful outcomes at a high level. There exists an algorithm called Boids invented by Craig Reynolds which was created to simulate the flocking behavior of birds, but it can also be applied to fish. There are three rules that boids follow (Separation, Alignment, and Cohesion) but I'm not going to explain them all in great detail here.

In its simplest form the boids algorithm possesses an inherent inefficiency as its time complexity is O(n2). Each boid has to check against every other boid. My solution to this is running the boids on the GPU via a compute shader. First I'll get into why that's better in terms of efficiency, and then I'll talk about why it's not the best.

Firstly, the GPU is massively parallelized, which means that we can rewrite our code to run one boid on each GPU process. This means that the time complexity for one process is O(n) and because all the processes are parallelized the time complexity stays at O(n) for the entire boids simulation. Unfortunately, it's not quite that simple because we can't just run infinite processes, there's a limit. This means our time complexity is still linear but can be more precisely defined as O(n/m) where m is the number of processes that can be run at once. On my machine that's 512.

Now it's safe to say that at this point the boid simulation is very efficient and is certainly not the bottleneck anymore. The main problem is that in order to make the boids avoid walls I have two choices: Figure out how to get Godot's signed distance field into the compute shader, or suck up the performance cost and do a thousand raycasts on the CPU (in the main thread). So I chose the latter.

Another bottleneck in this code is waiting for the GPU to process all the data and get it back to the CPU, which stalls the main thread. The best solution to this would probably be to rewrite everything as a particle shader, but I decided to go with the naïve solution which was to move the compute shader initialization to a separate thread, which luckily works with Godot. This was surprisingly easy to make, and I don't have much to say about it.

Creating Music for our Game

By Alejandro B: May 3, 2025

This blog post is going to cover my process for creating music for our game. In previous years, I have used the online tool BeepBox, and other mods of it such as JummBox and UltraBox, to make music for our games. While these tools are great, I feel like I'm not very good at using them for anything other than chiptune-style music, and this year, we decided to steer our music direction away from chiptune and electronic-sounding music in general.

Outside of video game music, as a composer of classical music I mainly use the music notation software Sibelius to write music. (Nowadays, Sibelius requires an expensive paid subscription to use its full version, but I've been able to get away with using a slightly older version of Sibelius with a permanent license from the olden days.) Sibelius also has quite good audio playback, and lets you export audio of your music. Since Sibelius is the software I'm most familiar with, and it would work well for the type of music we wanted for our game, I decided to use Sibelius to create this year's game's music.

Despite the time constraints brought around by our school schedule this year, I think the decisions we made concerning our game's music led to some of the best music I've ever made for any of our games, and I hope that you'll enjoy it too.
